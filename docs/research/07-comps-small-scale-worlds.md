# Research 07 — Comps: Mouse-Scale / Tiny-Creature Worlds

**Scope:** Ghost of a Tale, Moss 1 & 2, Tinykin, Grounded, It Takes Two (miniature chapters), plus Pikmin 4, The Wild at Heart, and diorama-presentation techniques (tilt-shift, Link's Awakening, Captain Toad).
**Date:** 2026-07-04. Sources inline. Where a number is community-measured rather than developer-stated, it is flagged.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **There are two distinct "tiny" fantasies and they need different rules.** *Shrunken-human worlds* (Grounded, Tinykin, It Takes Two) sell scale with oversized human props; *small-creature civilizations* (Ghost of a Tale, Moss, Brambly Hedge) build architecture at mouse ergonomics and let **nature at real-world size** be the giant. Brambly Hedge is firmly the second type — grass, cow parsley and the oak are your "oversized props," not spoons and hot dogs.
- **Camera height is the single strongest scale cue in a third-person game.** Pikmin 4's team only "got a sense of how small the Pikmin really were" when a prototype camera passed *under the slats of a wooden pallet with light shining through* — near-ground camera + towering occluders + light shafts is the canonical recipe ([Nintendo Ask the Developer Vol. 10 Pt. 3](https://www.nintendo.com/us/whatsnew/ask-the-developer-vol-10-pikmin-4-part-3/)).
- **Sound must be mixed by camera distance.** Pikmin 4 changes the mix with zoom: close to the ground you hear footsteps and voices "as if listening with your face close to the ground"; zoomed out you hear faint tiny-world noises under broad ambience ([Nintendo](https://www.nintendo.com/us/whatsnew/ask-the-developer-vol-10-pikmin-4-part-3/), [GoNintendo](https://gonintendo.com/contents/62323-pikmin-4-sound-designer-on-expressing-the-smallness-of-pikmin)). Moss made "all sounds larger relative to their geometric size" and scored the mouse with physically small instruments ([Game Developer](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/audio/using-vr-audio-to-bring-a-sense-of-scale-to-pint-sized-puzzler-i-moss-i-)).
- **The "giant empty floor" is solved three ways:** turn the floor itself into a forest (Grounded's grass), go vertical so the floor is only one of many surfaces (Tinykin's furniture cities), or shrink playable spaces to authored diorama rooms (Moss, Captain Toad). A hedgerow supports all three simultaneously.
- **Deliberate size ambiguity is healthy.** Miyamoto resisted showing Pikmin next to a battery because "it gives people an idea of its exact size. I don't feel entirely comfortable with this" ([Nintendo](https://www.nintendo.com/us/whatsnew/ask-the-developer-vol-10-pikmin-4-part-3/)). Barklem's books do the same — scale reads emotionally, not metrically.
- **Tiny worlds need giant landmarks.** Grounded put the oak tree "smack-dab [in the] middle of the backyard" for orientation (a Fallout: New Vegas lesson) ([Den of Geek](https://www.denofgeek.com/games/grounded-obsidian-interview-pc-xbox/)); Moss: Book II uses a castle visible from every diorama ([Road to VR](https://www.roadtovr.com/moss-book-2-behind-the-scenes-polyarc/)). Critical for us: journal-as-UI means no minimap, so the world must self-orient.
- **A tiny world is physically minuscule and that's a feature.** Grounded's entire backyard measures roughly **20 m × 20 m in real-world terms** (community measurement, [Steam discussion](https://steamcommunity.com/app/962130/discussions/0/3390672107116118789/)) yet takes **35+ minutes to creep across** ([howbigisthemap](https://howbigisthemap.com/grounded-creep-across-the-garden/)). A 20–40 m stretch of real hedgerow is a *huge* game world at mouse scale — density beats acreage.
- **Tilt-shift/diorama presentation has precise, copyable rules:** high camera angle, a sharp focus band with progressive blur toward screen top and bottom, raised contrast and saturation ("painted miniature under a light"), and faster motion (low inertia reads as small) ([Wikipedia: Miniature faking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miniature_faking)). Link's Awakening (2019) applies exactly this to feel like "looking into a miniature diorama" ([Zelda Dungeon](https://www.zeldadungeon.net/aonuma-explains-decision-to-utilize-tilt-shift-perspective-for-links-awakening/)).
- **Warning:** diorama DoF and "I am tiny" are opposing illusions. Tilt-shift makes the *viewer* feel like a giant looking at a toy; macro foreground blur makes the viewer feel small *inside* the scene. Moss deliberately chose the diorama feel (player = Reader looking in); Pikmin/Grounded chose immersion. Brambly Hedge's storybook camera must pick per context (see Implications).

## 1. Ghost of a Tale — a rodent civilization, one artist's "movie set"

The closest structural comp to Brambly Hedge: a mouse (Tilo) in a world built *by and for* rodents, not a shrunken human in ours.

- **Team/tooling:** ~90% made solo by ex-DreamWorks/Illumination animation director Lionel Gallat (plus ~4 part-time helpers: writer, programmer, composer, audio), in **Unity** after abandoning CryEngine; Maya + ZBrush pipeline with one-click export ([80.lv](https://80.lv/articles/ghost-of-a-tale-journey-from-minions-to-mice), [Game Developer](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/former-film-animator-discusses-making-the-gorgeous-i-ghost-of-a-tale-i-)). Direct proof a 1–3 person team can ship a lush mouse world in Unity.
- **Scale inside a same-species world:** scale contrast comes from *other creatures*, not humans — Dwindling Heights keep is "manned by Rats which are almost twice as tall as Mice," and the keep is built to rat proportions, so the mouse constantly compensates for his height, hides inside chests, and slips through gaps rats can't ([80.lv](https://80.lv/articles/ghost-of-a-tale-journey-from-minions-to-mice)).
- **Nature as the giant:** "every blade of grass or flower that towers above the mouse feels alive… what the developer does brilliantly with this world is present a sense of scale" ([Fandom review](https://www.fandom.com/articles/ghost-of-a-tale-stunning-achievement)).
- **Art economy:** Gallat's stated formula — "simple models and complex materials work best"; heavy use of camera post-effects: "using post-effects components on the camera allows me to effectively become a director of photography"; environments designed "a bit like movie sets," characters stylized ([80.lv](https://80.lv/articles/ghost-of-a-tale-journey-from-minions-to-mice), [Game Developer](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/former-film-animator-discusses-making-the-gorgeous-i-ghost-of-a-tale-i-)).
- **World structure:** one coherent interconnected place, "all the zones lead into each-other if you know how to open shortcuts (that's something I really liked in Dark Souls)" ([80.lv](https://80.lv/articles/ghost-of-a-tale-journey-from-minions-to-mice)).
- **Known weakness to avoid:** underground/interior lighting was criticized as "a little too deep" in shadow ([Game Developer](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/former-film-animator-discusses-making-the-gorgeous-i-ghost-of-a-tale-i-)) — cozy games can't afford murk.

## 2. Moss 1 & 2 — the diorama codified

- **Origin of the mouse:** Polyarc started from "diorama-size puzzles you interact with in 3rd person," and the diorama *led to* the mouse, not vice versa; sitting at human height looking into a small manipulable world felt empowering, whereas sitting *inside* life-sized VR made players feel "small and insignificant" ([oprainfall interview](https://operationrainfall.com/2021/01/27/vr-interview-polyarc-games-talks-about-moss-part-1/), [GDC Vault](https://gdcvault.com/play/1025604/VR-Interaction-Design-in-39)).
- **Camera grammar:** one **stationary, authored camera per room-scale diorama**, fade-out/fade-in between scenes so players never lose orientation; "by casting the player as a benevolent and proactive camera, the developers freed themselves from the eternal complexities that a third-person platforming camera always creates" ([UploadVR](https://www.uploadvr.com/moss-vr-magical-preview/)). Quill is real-mouse-sized; the world is "crumbling ruins you could fit in a shoebox" ([Meta blog](https://www.meta.com/blog/moss-anniversary-quill-polyarc-discount-vr-bundle/)).
- **Two scales in one frame:** Book II dioramas keep both readings alive — Quill navigates individual grass blades and pebbles while the player simultaneously sees "the whole tree the branch is connected to, and the hill where the rocks fell from"; dioramas were deliberately visually connected so players could place themselves in the world, anchored by a castle hub visible from afar ([Road to VR](https://www.roadtovr.com/moss-book-2-behind-the-scenes-polyarc/)).
- **Audio scale:** audio director Stephen Hodde made "all sounds larger relative to their geometric size" and mixed toward Quill's emotional perspective; composer Jason Graves picked instruments "small in size and sound to relate to Quill" — ukulele, hammered dulcimer, Celtic harp; Quill's injured heartbeat was recorded with a fetal doppler ([Game Developer](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/audio/using-vr-audio-to-bring-a-sense-of-scale-to-pint-sized-puzzler-i-moss-i-); GDC talk ["Moss: Design Before Sound"](https://www.gdcvault.com/play/1026324/-Moss-Design-Before)).
- **Animation:** film-quality hand animation of ear/whisker/breathing micro-motion is what made players bond with a 10 cm character (GDC: "Animating Quill," [PlayStation Blog](https://blog.playstation.com/2018/04/09/the-magic-of-animation-in-moss-how-polyarc-brought-quill-to-life/)).

## 3. Tinykin — verticality kills the empty floor

- **Structure:** six rooms of one 1990s house, each a self-contained, **highly vertical** level hosting a themed insect civilization — bathroom as pool-party city, kitchen as farmland, bedroom as amusement park "with a castle, a racetrack… while a giant dragon oversees it all" ([SUPERJUMP](https://www.superjumpmagazine.com/the-joyful-design-of-tinykin/), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tinykin)).
- **Furniture is the terrain:** shelves, drawers and cupboards are the platforming; "opening cupboards and drawers reveals the hidden contents within, which expand the level" — interiors of furniture become sub-levels; the team cited Dark Souls-style interconnection ([Gamerant interview](https://gamerant.com/tinykin-splashteam-interview-design-e3-2021/), [NeoGAF thread](https://www.neogaf.com/threads/tinykin-a-great-3d-platformer.1649363/)).
- **Design difficulty of mundane spaces:** director Romain Claude admitted "all the things you find in the house are kind of boring. The point is to twist today's life into funny things you can play with" ([Gamerant](https://gamerant.com/tinykin-splashteam-interview-design-e3-2021/)). Influences: Pikmin + *Honey, I Shrunk the Kids*.
- **Density model:** "densely packed with objectives, collectibles and whimsical places"; constant "low-stakes but high-frequency" pickups (pollen/nectar trails) keep every traversal rewarding; movement is a soap-board glide plus bubble float, no fall damage — friction-free traversal for a low-stress game ([SUPERJUMP](https://www.superjumpmagazine.com/the-joyful-design-of-tinykin/)). Reviewers: Splashteam made "the most mundane of household rooms look more expansive and exciting to explore than a map from Skyrim" ([WayTooManyGames](https://waytoomany.games/2022/08/22/review-tinykin/)).
- **Stylization trick:** all characters are 2D sprites that always face the camera against 3D environments — cheap to produce, instantly charming, "helps set the stage and immerse the player in each level's fantasy" ([SUPERJUMP](https://www.superjumpmagazine.com/the-joyful-design-of-tinykin/)).

## 4. Grounded — the floor is a forest; hard numbers

- **Scale numbers:** shrunken teens are ~7 mm–1 cm tall (community wiki lists "a mere 7 millimeters," [Grounded wiki](https://grounded.fandom.com/wiki/Teens)); the whole backyard is ~**20 m × 20 m** of real-world lawn (player measurement using in-game units, [Steam discussion](https://steamcommunity.com/app/962130/discussions/0/3390672107116118789/)); crossing it corner-to-corner at creep speed takes **~35:42** ([howbigisthemap](https://howbigisthemap.com/grounded-creep-across-the-garden/)). Effective scale factor ≈ 1:200 — a lawn becomes a multi-hour open world.
- **Grass = forest:** the founding design goal was "a huge grass forest"; grass blades are individually simulated cover — big creatures displace them so "you see the grass shaking in the distance," a deliberate Jurassic Park moment ([Den of Geek](https://www.denofgeek.com/games/grounded-obsidian-interview-pc-xbox/)). This is the definitive answer to the giant-empty-floor problem for outdoor micro worlds: **the ground plane itself becomes a dense, occluding, navigable biome.**
- **Navigation:** central oak tree placed mid-map as an always-visible orientation landmark, explicitly borrowing from Fallout: New Vegas's Lucky 38 ([Den of Geek](https://www.denofgeek.com/games/grounded-obsidian-interview-pc-xbox/)).
- **Familiarity as wonder:** discovery beats are ordinary objects re-scaled — "a half-eaten hot dog, or a figurine, or a toy"; a suburban backyard was chosen because everyone already knows it, so the warped scale alone supplies the wonder ([Den of Geek](https://www.denofgeek.com/games/grounded-obsidian-interview-pc-xbox/), [GameSpot](https://www.gamespot.com/articles/grounded-director-talks-about-building-a-world-for-the-story-to-play-inside/1100-6483740/)).
- **Sequel scaling:** Grounded 2's park starts at the size of Grounded 1's entire yard and targets ~3× — driven by mounts increasing traversal speed ([GamesRadar](https://www.gamesradar.com/games/survival/everything-is-bigger-in-grounded-2-but-obsidian-never-considered-stretching-beyond-four-player-co-op-it-would-have-undermined-what-grounded-was-really-about/)). Lesson: world size must follow movement speed, not ambition.

## 5. It Takes Two — miniature chapters as authored set-pieces

- Doll-sized protagonists traverse seven chapters, most of them miniature-scale domestic/garden spaces: The Shed, The Tree, Rose's Room, Cuckoo Clock, Snow Globe, Garden, The Attic ([chapter guide](https://www.exitlag.com/blog/it-takes-two-chapters-guide/), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/It_Takes_Two_(video_game))).
- **Art rule:** Hazelight's environment artists focused on "scale, shape and a stylized approach but without losing the realism," with **tiling textures selling the materials** at close range — wood grain, fabric weave and rust become landscape when the character is 10 cm tall ([ArtStation Magazine Art Blast](https://magazine.artstation.com/2021/04/hazelight-studios-it-takes-two-art-blast/)).
- **Structure lesson:** rather than one open miniature world, each chapter is a linear gauntlet where "major mechanics change regularly rather than repeating from level to level," tied to the chapter's theme (magnets in the shed, sap/matches in the tree) ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/It_Takes_Two_(video_game))). Miniature scale is used as *set-piece variety*, not systemic simulation — the anti-Grounded. For a systemic cozy game this is mostly a counter-example, but its garden chapter is the best-in-class visual reference for "wet soil, roots and plants as cathedral."

## 6. Pikmin 4 — camera and audio rules from Nintendo

The most quotable primary source on *manufacturing smallness* ([Ask the Developer Vol. 10, Pt. 3](https://www.nintendo.com/us/whatsnew/ask-the-developer-vol-10-pikmin-4-part-3/)):

- **Near-ground camera:** "By setting the camera angle at a near-ground level, you'll look up at the objects in the environment and see things from a Pikmin's perspective instead of a human's." Player camera can go under benches and desks.
- **The pallet epiphany:** the team (who had made three Pikmin games) only *felt* the scale when a prototype passed under wooden pallet slats with "a ray of light shining through it nicely." Overhead structure + light shafts = smallness.
- **Size ambiguity:** Miyamoto rejected placing Pikmin next to a battery — exact size comparison kills the fantasy.
- **Zoom-linked audio mix:** camera low = Pikmin voices/footsteps "loud and clear, as if listening with your face close to the ground"; zoomed out = "faint noises from the world of tiny creatures spreading out below" under ambient sound.
- **Footstep recipe:** tiny footsteps that still feel responsive = "for the first half we used human footsteps, and for the second half we used the sound of fingers crushing gravel"; plus material-specific foley (cardboard, aluminium foil) — "feel good to the touch first, then think of a way to bring out size" ([GoNintendo](https://gonintendo.com/contents/62323-pikmin-4-sound-designer-on-expressing-the-smallness-of-pikmin)).

## 7. The Wild at Heart — storybook art at critter scale

- 2D hand-drawn "illustrative storybook" characters/props over 3D-lit environments; Unity + Photoshop + Spine; influences Song of the Sea, Where the Wild Things Are, Ghibli — "a comfortable, yet haunting feel" ([Game Developer](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/turning-personal-art-into-wild-at-heart), [Red Bull interview](https://www.redbull.com/us-en/moonlight-kids-the-wild-at-heart-interview)).
- Scale is conveyed almost entirely through art direction (dense hand-drawn undergrowth, oversized junk like an abandoned TV used for environmental storytelling) rather than camera tricks — evidence that an illustration-first pipeline can sell "small creatures in a big forest" without simulation. Closest tonal comp for Barklem's cross-hatched hedgerow density.

## 8. Diorama presentation: tilt-shift, Link's Awakening, Captain Toad

- **Tilt-shift physics ([Wikipedia: Miniature faking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miniature_faking)):** (1) shoot from a **high angle** "to simulate the effect of looking down on a miniature"; (2) blur "progressively… from the center to the top and center to the bottom" — a sharp horizontal focus band; (3) **increase contrast** (hard shadows of a model under a lamp) and **saturation** (painted miniature); (4) run motion faster — "this appears to reduce the inertia which would normally limit the motion of large objects." Mechanism: DoF blur is a distance cue; injecting close-up blur "overrides this familiar information causing objects to appear miniature and toy-like."
- **Link's Awakening (2019):** whole game rendered with tilt-shift "blurring the extreme foreground and background… to enhance the effect of looking at an elaborate diorama"; Aonuma chose it to recapture the "small… but very vast" world seen on the Game Boy's 66 mm screen; paired with glossy, toy-like character materials ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Link%27s_Awakening_(2019_video_game)), [Zelda Dungeon](https://www.zeldadungeon.net/aonuma-explains-decision-to-utilize-tilt-shift-perspective-for-links-awakening/)).
- **Captain Toad:** each level is a rotatable diorama; the concept came from a Mario test where "if Mario could jump, the stages would become pretty big," so a non-jumping hero kept spaces diorama-sized; tunnels and pillars deliberately force camera rotation so players learn the mechanic ([Nintendo Life interview](https://www.nintendolife.com/news/2014/12/interview_captain_toad_treasure_trackers_developers_talk_over_origins_and_the_contents_of_toads_backpack)). Directly relevant to a limited-rotation storybook camera: **author sightline occluders that reward the rotation you do allow.**
- **VR-specific note (Moss context):** in VR, perceived scale is governed by virtual IPD, viewpoint height and movement speed relative to body size — "by merely manipulating the IPD, we can make the whole world shrink or grow" ([Sense of Scale in VR](https://kholdstare.github.io/technical/2013/10/06/sense-of-scale-vr.html)). Flat-screen equivalents: camera height, FOV, parallax speed and familiar-object references ([Game Developer: proportions and scale](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/design-tips-in-game-proportions-and-scale)).

## Implications for Brambly Hedge: The Game

**Our position in the genre:** Brambly Hedge is a *mouse-civilization* world (Ghost of a Tale/Moss family), not a shrunken-human world. The wonder is not "the spoon is huge" — it is "the mice have made a civilization inside something we walk past." Two scale registers must coexist in every frame: **mouse-made things at mouse ergonomics** (crockery, stairs inside the Palace tree, the mill machinery) and **nature at true macro scale** (stems, leaves, roots, the hedge itself).

### Concrete rules (opinionated)

1. **Fix a canonical character height and derive everything.** Set the player mouse at 10 cm ≈ 1 unity-unit-equivalent reference. Prop ratio table: mouse door 1.2–1.4× character height; mouse table ~0.5×; grass blades 3–6× (30–60 cm); cow parsley/hogweed 15–20×; bramble canes 8–12× and arching overhead; the oak/mill effectively "skybox-scale" landmarks. Blackberries slightly larger than the character's head; a daisy bloom = parasol. Follow Grounded/Tinykin in *exaggerating* gameplay-relevant props ~1.2–1.5× over botanical truth for readability, and follow Miyamoto in never stating exact size in-fiction.
2. **Camera: keep the storybook isometric as default, but build "pallet moments."** Default semi-iso 35–45° reads as diorama (good — it's the Tiny Glade/Link's Awakening register). At key locations and at max zoom-in, drop the pivot to ~1–2 character heights above ground so the player looks *up* through overhanging stems with sun shafts (Pikmin's pallet epiphany). These low authored framings are where "I am a mouse" lands; the high framings are where "what a lovely miniature world" lands. Both are on-brand; script which one each scene wants.
3. **Zoom-linked audio mix (copy Pikmin 4 wholesale).** Bind a mixer snapshot to camera zoom: zoomed in = intimate foley (footsteps, cloth, breathing, close leaf rustle), narrowed ambience; zoomed out = wide ambience with faint tiny-world details below. Footsteps: layer a crisp responsive transient with a micro-texture tail (their "fingers crushing gravel" trick) and author per-material variants (leaf litter, bark, flagstone, floorboard, moss). Score with physically small instruments à la Moss — harp, dulcimer, recorder, celeste.
4. **Never ship a bare ground plane.** Rule of thumb: no walkable exterior surface should extend more than ~4–5 character lengths (~0.5 m real scale) without micro-cover — grass clumps, fallen leaves (each leaf is a rug), pebbles, roots, seed heads, snail shells. Where the design needs open space (festival green), justify it in-fiction as *mouse-maintained* (mown, swept) and ring it with dense verge. Interiors: Tinykin's rule — furniture is terrain; every shelf, drawer and stair inside the tree-homes should be reachable and hiding something small.
5. **Exploit the hedge's native verticality.** A hedgerow is a layer cake: root cellars/burrows → field floor → stem lattice → branch canopy. Treat stems and thorn spirals as ramps/stairs (traversal, not platforming); treat small gaps (a ditch, a cart rut, a stream) as chasms crossed by community-built bridges — which conveniently feeds the community-projects pillar (repairing a twig bridge changes NPC routes, like Grounded's grass paths).
6. **De-emphasize jumping.** Captain Toad's lesson: a non-jumping (or barely-jumping) character keeps spaces small, dense and readable, and spares you the third-person platforming camera problem Moss also dodged. A mouse that climbs, squeezes and scampers fits Brambly Hedge fiction better than one that jumps.
7. **Landmark discipline instead of a minimap.** With journal-as-UI, adopt Grounded/Moss navigation: one mega-landmark visible from almost everywhere (the Palace oak or the mill chimney with smoke), plus per-region silhouettes (crab-apple tree, cornfield edge). Test: from any exterior point, at default zoom, at least one landmark must break the canopy line.
8. **Tilt-shift: use a focus band, not a toy filter.** Implement DoF with a horizontal focus band on the character plane, progressive blur toward top/bottom of frame, mildly raised saturation/contrast — but keep the effect subtle in normal play and strongest in the cinematic framings and photo mode (a "storybook plate" look). Avoid full-strength tilt-shift during mouse-eye moments — it fights the being-small illusion (it tells the player they're a giant Reader). On Unity built-in pipeline, Post-Processing Stack v2 DoF approximates this; a proper tilted focal plane / band blur is a small custom shader — prototype early, since this look influences all environment dressing.
9. **Texture like Hazelight, model like Gallat.** At mouse scale, material detail *is* the landscape: invest in high-frequency tiling textures (bark ridges, moss sponge, wood grain, wool weave) over dense geometry — "simple models and complex materials," with camera post as the director of photography. This is also the only sane budget for a 1–3 person team, per Ghost of a Tale's existence proof.
10. **Size the world in real-hedgerow meters, not game-world hectares.** Grounded gets 30+ hours from a 20 m × 20 m yard. A hedgerow strip of ~30–50 real meters (≈ 3–5 km of perceived mouse-scale distance), two fields' edges and a stream is more than enough for the whole game. Spend the budget on density (a POI, interaction or charming vignette every 5–10 seconds of walking, Tinykin-style, tuned gentler) and on seasonal re-dressing of the *same* space — four seasons quadruples your one hedgerow.
11. **Animate small = fast + busy.** Miniature-faking shows faster motion reads as smaller (less apparent inertia). Give the mouse a quick footstep cadence, darting head/ear/whisker idle micro-motion (Quill's playbook), and let big things (the heron, wind through the canopy, a passing cow) move *slowly* for contrast. The speed differential between mouse and world is itself a scale cue.

### What to copy / what to avoid

| Copy | From | Avoid | From |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zoom-linked audio mixing; footstep layering | Pikmin 4 | Stating exact character size in fiction | anti-Miyamoto |
| Grass-as-forest ground cover; central mega-landmark | Grounded | Survival-scale empty lawns between POIs | Grounded's weaker stretches |
| Furniture-as-terrain interiors; high-frequency pickups | Tinykin | Combat/threat framing of wildlife | Grounded, GoaT stealth |
| Authored per-room camera framings, fade transitions | Moss, Captain Toad | Free-rotate orbit camera everywhere | generic 3P games |
| "Simple models, complex materials," post-FX as cinematography | Ghost of a Tale | Over-dark interiors | GoaT's criticized dungeons |
| Tiling-texture material storytelling at macro scale | It Takes Two | One-shot set-piece level design (unsystemic) | It Takes Two |
| Tilt-shift focus band + saturation for storybook plates | Link's Awakening | Permanent heavy tilt-shift during mouse-eye play | — |
